Historical Information

Ho Chi Minh City, previously referred to as Saigon, had a lengthy and illustrious history that originates from the early ages. The Khmer people first settled the city as a little fishing village prior to its incorporation into the Champa Kingdom. Vietnamese settlers started their migration southward in the 17th century, founding the region known as Saigon. Throughout the French colonial era, Saigon evolved as the economic and cultural center of French Indochina, developing into a vibrant port city throughout time.

The French influence remains apparent in Ho Chi Minh City’s architecture, especially in notable structures such as the Notre Dame Cathedral and the Central Post Office. These structures represent a fusion of Gothic and Renaissance architectural traditions, juxtaposed with contemporary skyscrapers. Saigon functioned as the capital of South Vietnam and was pivotal in the Vietnam War. The conflict concluded with the Fall of Saigon in 1975, leading to the revolutionary leader Ho Chi Minh's influence in renaming the city to Ho Chi Minh City.

Currently, Ho Chi Minh City is the most populous city in Vietnam, with over 9 million residents, and continues to serve as a crucial economic and cultural hub. The city's history is encapsulated in many museums, notably the War Remnants Museum , which provides a poignant examination of the Vietnam War's consequences.


How To Get There

Accessing Ho Chi Minh City is very uncomplicated, as it serves as a significant international center in Southeast Asia. Tan Son Nhat International Airport (SGN), the primary airport, is situated at 7 kilometers from the city center and provides both international and local flights. It is interconnected with major global cities, including direct flights from locations such as New York, Paris, and Tokyo.

Upon arrival, passengers may conveniently access the city via taxi or ride-hailing services such as Grab, which are prevalent in Vietnam. The airport has shuttle buses that operate straight to District 1, the city's core, where the majority of tourists prefer to reside.

For those traveling within Vietnam, there are numerous domestic aircraft, trains, and buses link Ho Chi Minh City with other regions of Vietnam. The Reunification Express operates between Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City, providing a cost-effective and picturesque journey across the nation.

Navigating the city may be a considerable experience. Although public buses are available, the majority of visitors use taxis, motorbike taxis, or ride-hailing applications. Motorbikes prevail on the streets of Ho Chi Minh City, and hiring one offers a thrilling means of exploration; nevertheless, one must be ready for the tumultuous traffic.


Where To Stay

Ho Chi Minh City has a diverse array of lodgings, ranging from economical hostels to opulent hotels, catering to all types of travelers. District 1 is the most sought-after region for accommodation, recognized for its abundance of hotels, restaurants, and activities. District 1 has locations like as Pham Ngu Lao Street, which accommodates budget tourists by providing economical hostels and guesthouses. Backpackers congregate in this region because to its vibrant nightlife and closeness to the city's primary attractions.

The Dong Khoi area is an excellent option for individuals seeking more opulent lodgings. Upscale accommodations, such the Hotel Majestic Saigon and The Reverie Saigon, provide breathtaking vistas of the Saigon River and exceptional facilities. Numerous hotels are situated within proximity to notable sights such as the Saigon Opera House and Notre Dame Cathedral.

For a more tranquil and local experience, District 3 is an excellent option. This region is less frequented by tourists nevertheless has a commendable array of boutique accommodations and local dining establishments, facilitating a more genuine Vietnamese experience.


What And Where To Eat

Ho Chi Minh City is a culinary haven, celebrated for its dynamic street food scene and authentic Vietnamese gastronomy. A visit to the city would be incomplete without sampling the local cuisine, which often embodies a fusion of French colonial influence and Vietnamese cuisines.

One of the most iconic dishes to try is Pho, a flavorful noodle soup made with beef or chicken, herbs, and spices. While pho can be found all over Vietnam, some of the best bowls are served in the early morning at local street stalls throughout the city. Another must-try dish is Banh Mi, a French-inspired Vietnamese sandwich made with a crispy baguette filled with pork, pate, pickled vegetables, and herbs.

For a more refined dining experience, District 1 boasts a variety of restaurants offering both traditional and contemporary Vietnamese food. The Ben Thanh Market is an excellent destination for street food enthusiasts to experience a diverse array of Vietnamese cuisine, such as Com tam (broken rice with barbecued pork), Banh Xeo (crispy pancakes), and Fresh Spring Rolls.


Local Attractions

Ho Chi Minh City offers a diverse range of attractions for both history enthusiasts and casual visitors. A visit to the War Remnants Museum is an moving experience that offers insight into the Vietnam War and its repercussions on the nation. The museum has images, military artifacts, and personal narratives from the battle, rendering it one of the most frequented locations in the city.

Another essential feature is the Notre Dame Cathedral, constructed in the late 19th century during French colonial governance. The cathedral's neo-Romanesque style and dual bell towers constitute a distinctive element of the city's skyline. Adjacent to the cathedral lies the Central Post Office, an architectural masterpiece created by the renowned French architect Gustave Eiffel.

Ben Thanh Market is the premier destination for shopping. This vibrant market provides a diverse array of fresh vegetables, street cuisine, apparel, and souvenirs. It is an excellent location to hone your negotiation abilities and engage with the local culture.


Interesting Events

Ho Chi Minh City is a hub of festivals and festivities, with events throughout the year. Tet Nguyen Dan, the Vietnamese Lunar New Year, is one of the most major holidays. During Tet, the city vibrantly transforms with decorations, fireworks, and traditional entertainment, as families convene to commemorate the start of the new year. The Ao Dai Festival is a vibrant celebration that honors the ancient Vietnamese garment, the Ao Dai. The event includes fashion displays, cultural performances, and exhibitions, attracting tourists nationwide.

Whether you're exploring its rich history, indulging in its delicious cuisine, or taking part in one of its many festivals, Ho Chi Minh City offers an unforgettable experience for every traveler.

Popular Folklore of Ho Chi Minh City

Ho Chi Minh City, like much of Vietnam, is steeped in myths and legends that reflect the country’s deep cultural roots and beliefs. Here are some popular folktales and legends that give insight into the city’s unique cultural identity:

  1. The Tale of the Turtle Lake:

    • Ho Con Rua (Turtle Lake) is a famous gathering spot in Ho Chi Minh City with a mysterious past. Although not an actual lake, it’s a landmark known for its turtle-shaped fountain and beautiful architecture.

    • According to local legend, the lake and its surroundings are built over a spiritual nexus that protects the city. The turtle symbolizes longevity and protection, and the lake is said to hold mystical powers that help keep Ho Chi Minh City safe from harm. Many locals believe that touching the water or statues at Turtle Lake brings good luck.

  2. The Story of the Jade Emperor Pagoda:

    • The Jade Emperor Pagoda is a renowned location in Ho Chi Minh City, distinguished for its elaborate sculptures and spiritual importance. Folklore states that the pagoda is consecrated to the Jade Emperor, the sovereign of Heaven in Vietnamese and Chinese mythology. The location is said to be a transit point for spirits before to entering the afterlife.

    • A multitude of visitors to the pagoda beseech the Jade Emperor for blessings and protection. The pagoda is enveloped in stories and is regarded as a locus for spirit congregation, rendering it a spiritual nexus in the vibrant metropolis.
